Rating philosophy

The calibration baseline

3

A rating of 3 is strong performance

A 3 means the individual consistently meets expectations, delivers reliable outcomes, and operates independently at the expected scope.

4+

Ratings above 3 require sustained stretch

A 4 reflects consistently stronger outcomes within or adjacent to normal role scope. A 5 reflects sustained, role-defining contribution that materially raises the bar for peers.

2

Ratings below 3 reflect inconsistency

A 2 means expectations are only partially met. A 1 should be reserved for sustained, significant underperformance with prior feedback and follow-up.

Scoring judgement

When to test the next score

Supporting questions are evidence prompts, not a numeric checklist. Use them to decide whether to stay at the current anchor or test the next one up or down.

1

Start at 3

First decide whether the employee consistently met the expected role standard for this driver. If the answer is broadly yes, and there are no material gaps, 3 is the likely anchor.

2

Use yes answers as signals

If roughly 80% or more of the current anchor questions are supported by evidence, test the next score. If the missing answer relates to a critical expectation, stay at the lower anchor.

3

Raise only with stronger evidence

Move from 3 to 4 only when performance is sustained beyond expectations. Move from 4 to 5 only when contribution is exceptional, role-defining, and recognised beyond isolated moments.

4

Lower when gaps are material

Test 2 when expected behaviours or outcomes are inconsistent. Test 1 only when gaps are serious, sustained, and supported by clear feedback, intervention, and documented follow-up.

Example: If a manager answers 5 out of 6 questions as yes, that is a prompt to examine the next rating anchor. It is not an automatic score increase. The final rating depends on whether the missing question is role-critical and whether the higher anchor is supported by sustained, comparable evidence.

Evidence expectations

Ground ratings in observable patterns

Strong evidence is specific, role-relevant, comparable across employees, and connected to outcomes or behaviours over the review period.

Specific

Name the outcome, behaviour, context, timing, and impact.

Comparable

Use the same role-level criteria and similar evidence types across comparable employees.

Sustained

Look for patterns across the review period, not one recent or highly visible moment.

Relevant

Anchor the rating in work outcomes, judgement, behaviours, and role expectations.
